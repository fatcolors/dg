From chaos to clarity

DataGEMS is an AI data discovery platform that I designed from scratch. I chose this project because the hardest part was not the design. It was getting a very large, spread-out team to agree on the shape and structure of the system. I will spend one minute on the product, and the rest of the time on how I worked in this project — from research to hand-off.

2

What is DataGEMS?

DataGEMS helps people find and explore datasets. You can search for data in plain language, without writing complicated queries. Our end users are scientist but the platform will be available to all researchers. The problem it solves is simple: data is scattered everywhere and hard to find. It is an EU research project, built by many universities across Europe — so it was big and complex from day one.

3

One vision, many teams

I was the only designer, and I was responsible for the whole product. There were more than 70 people from different countries in Europe involved. Each team built a different part, and each team wanted something different. So my main job was not drawing screens. It was alignment — talking to every team, learning what they really needed, and bringing it all into one clear vision.

4

Research — listening, then aligning

I started with research, but a special kind: I listened to the teams to get them all on the same page and align their expectations. I met each group to learn what they truly needed. Then I mapped where the needs matched, where there were gaps, and where they did not agree. I took all of this and turned it into one clear, ranked list of priorities. That gave everyone a single plan to design and build from.

5

Mapping a shorter path

From those priorities, I built user-flow diagrams. They covered search, data profiling, and the AI recommendations. The flows helped everyone agree on how the product should work, before we designed any screens. And I kept improving them to remove extra steps. Fewer steps mean less friction, and a faster way to reach the right data.

6

Fast, simple decisions

Next I made simple, low-detail wireframes. I kept them rough on purpose. The goal here was the layout and the main actions, not the visuals. That helped us make the big decisions early, before anyone got attached to a polished design.

7

A clickable vision

Then I turned the wireframes into a clickable prototype. This was the best tool for alignment on the whole project. Instead of arguing about ideas, people could click through a real product and react to it. This showed us problems early — while they were still easy and cheap to fix, and before any code was written.

8

A guide devs can build from

For the developers, I prepared a clear style guide, with all the flows, screens, and components documented. I also designed every screen for all sizes, from large desktop down to mobile. And for each new feature, I recorded a short video showing how it works. I shared these videos with the whole team, from scientists to developers, to keep everyone on the same page.

9

A build-ready product

The result was a working app, ready for its users. Because the documents were clear and the big decisions were made early, the build was faster and had fewer errors. My main lesson: in a large team, clarity is the real deliverable — often more than any single screen and good documentation is simply design that scales.
